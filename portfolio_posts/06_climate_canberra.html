This script downloads NCEI temperature data from the Canberra, Australia airport and makes some figures to evaluate trends over time.

Australia is a special place for me¶

I completed my PhD work in Australia where I was based out of Canberra and a tiny town called Bombala in southeastern Australia. The timber industry is very prevalent in this area. In the 1980s, the state forest service cleared native Eucalyptus forest and replaced it with a Monterrey pine plantation for timber harvest. However, they left behind fragments of native forest of different sizes and distances to one another. I examined how species at high trophic levels in the invertebrate food web, e.g., predator ground beetles and nematode parasites, responded to habitat fragmentation and then wildfire.

In addition to habitat loss and fragmentation, southeastern Australia faces many threats including climate change. Habitat fragmentation can excerbate these threats by introducing more edge habitat and decreasing connectivity to larger, continuous habitats.

Data description I accessed daily climate summaries data using the NCEI API for the Canberra Airport in Canberra, Australian Capitol Territory, Australia. The Global Historical Climatology Network (GHCN)-Daily dataset contains records from 80,000 stations across 180 countries and territories. Data collected includes maximum and minimum temperature, precipitation, snowfall, and snow depth. Most stations report precipitation only, however.

Menne, M. J., I. Durre, R. S. Vose, B. E. Gleason, & T. G. Houston (2012). An Overview of the Global Historical Climatology Network-Daily Database. Journal of Atmospheric and Oceanic Technology, 29, 897–910. https://doi.org/10.1175/JTECH-D-11-00103.1

NOAA NCEI. Daily Summaries API, station ASN00070014 (Canberra Airport, Australia), TMAX, 1938-2010. Accessed 2025-10-01.

Data analysis using an OLS linear regression¶

Next, I used an OLS linear regression to determine the rate of increase (i.e., the slope of the regression line). Maximum average annual temperatures are increasing by 0.02 degrees Celcius per year.

In [ ]:
# Fit an OLS linear regression


# Reset index so DATE is a column again
can_ann_df = can_ann_df.reset_index()

# Extract year
can_ann_df["year"] = can_ann_df["DATE"].dt.year

# Drop rows with missing temp values
can_ann_df = can_ann_df.dropna(subset=["max_temp_c"])

# Define predictors (X) and response (y)
X = can_ann_df["year"].values.reshape(-1, 1)   # sklearn needs 2D array
y = can_ann_df["max_temp_c"].values

# Fit linear regression
model = LinearRegression()
model.fit(X, y)

# Save coefficients
slope = model.coef_[0]
intercept = model.intercept_

# Print coefficients
print("Intercept (β0):", model.intercept_)
print("Slope (β1, °C per year):", model.coef_[0])

Finally, I visualize results in a nice plot and make predictions into the future using the model. An important assumption when making these predictions is that the climate continues to warm at a consistent rate and other factors are not influencing the rising trend that we see. This assumption is likely not valid, but this is still a useful thought exercise (and good Python practice!).

Conclusions¶

Average annual maximum temperatures in Canberra, Australia have increased at a rate of 0.02°C per year from 1939 to 2010 - a difference of 1.56°C. Using the model to make predictions into the future, the estimated average maximum temperature will be 21.29°C in 2050 - 0.88°C higher than what was observed in 2010.

According to the New South Wales government, by 2050, the predicted average temperature in the Australian Capital Territory (ACT) is expected to be 1.1°C higher than it is now under a low-emissions scenario and 1.8°C higher under a high-emissions scenario, which means my model actually underestimates the effects of climate change. Remarkably, the ACT is already 100% powered by renewable energy and has multiple climate change, infrastructure, and integrated energy strategies going into the future. Notably, targets include achieving 30% canopy cover and 30% surface permeability across Canberra's urban areas by 2045.

Similiar to the United States, climate change has contributed to unprecedented wildfires, heatwaves, floods, droughts, and climate-related infectious diseases across Australia. While climate-forward looking strategies are important, it is crucial to develop some of these strategies into actions to address climate change impacts.
